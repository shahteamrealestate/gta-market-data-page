Common questions

GTA housing market data — FAQ

What does Months of Inventory (MOI) mean, and is the GTA a buyer's or seller's market right now?

Months of Inventory (MOI) measures how long it would take to sell every active listing at the current pace of sales, assuming no new homes came to market. On this dashboard, under 2.5 months signals a seller's market, 2.5–4 months is balanced, and above 4 months favours buyers. As of June 2026, the GTA sits at approximately 4.7 months — in buyer-favouring territory. Use the dashboard above to see the current MOI for any specific city. Full explanation of TRREB terms →

What does the sale-to-list price ratio (SP/LP) tell me about negotiating power?

The sale-to-list price ratio shows what percentage of final asking price homes are actually selling for, on average. A ratio of 100% means homes sell exactly at asking. Above 100% means competitive multiple offers. In June 2026, the GTA average was approximately 98% — meaning sale prices came in about 2% below the final asking price on average. Actual results vary by home, condition and negotiation, so this isn't a guaranteed discount on any specific home. Current GTA market conditions →

How many days on market is normal in Brampton and Mississauga right now?

In June 2026, the GTA average was 29 days on market. Days on market varies by city, property type and season — use the dashboard above to check the current figure for your specific area and home type. As a general rule, listings sitting well beyond the local average often have more room to negotiate. Read the latest GTA market update →

How should I compare average price, inventory and days on market instead of looking at one number?

Do not look at one number alone. Average price shows the broad direction of the market. Inventory shows how much choice buyers have — more inventory generally means more room to negotiate. Days on market shows how quickly homes are moving. Together these three numbers give a much more realistic picture than any one of them alone.

What's the difference between average price and the MLS® HPI benchmark price?

Average price is simply the total dollar volume divided by the number of sales. It can be skewed by a few very expensive or very cheap transactions. The MLS® HPI benchmark price is a quality-adjusted measure that tracks a “typical” home over time. It's a better indicator of real price trends. This dashboard uses the HPI benchmark for price charts.

Will GTA home prices drop further in 2026?

Most major forecasters expect the GTA to post modest low-to-mid single-digit price gains in 2026, with detached homes leading and condos lagging. As of June 2026, average prices remain about 4–7% below June 2025 depending on property type. The Bank of Canada held rates at 2.25% at its July 2026 decision, which supports gradual recovery. Over 100,000 sidelined buyers represent pent-up demand that could accelerate a rebound. Is this a good window for first-time buyers? →

How much household income do I need to buy a home in Brampton or Mississauga?

At June 2026 prices and current stress-test rates (roughly 7.25%), qualifying for a $900,000 home in Brampton with 20% down typically requires a combined household income of approximately $175,000–$195,000. For a $700,000 Brampton townhouse, the threshold drops to around $135,000–$150,000. These are estimates — lender, credit score, existing debts, and property type all affect your number. Should I buy a condo or a freehold home in the GTA in 2026?

Freeholds (detached, semi, townhouse) are recovering faster — sales are up and inventory is tightening. Condos are still in buyer's market territory, with 5–7 months of inventory in most GTA cities and prices down 5–9% year-over-year. If you're an end-user planning to stay 5+ years, today's condo pricing offers real value. What to know about condo fees before you buy →

How do Bank of Canada rate changes affect home prices in Brampton and Mississauga?

Rate cuts reduce monthly carrying costs, which expands the pool of qualified buyers and gradually pushes prices up. Each 0.25% cut adds roughly $15,000–$25,000 in purchasing power for a typical GTA buyer. The Economic Context section above tracks the current overnight rate, prime rate, and inflation. What closing costs should I budget for as a first-time buyer in Ontario?

Beyond your down payment, first-time buyers in Ontario typically need 1.5–4% of the purchase price in closing costs. For a $900,000 Brampton home, budget $13,500–$36,000 extra. The biggest items are Ontario Land Transfer Tax (first-time buyers get a rebate up to $4,000), legal fees, title insurance, and home inspection. Toronto buyers pay a second municipal LTT on top of Ontario's. Full closing cost breakdown →

How do I know if a home is priced fairly in today's GTA market?

Check three things: (1) the HPI benchmark price for that city and property type in this dashboard, (2) recent sold prices for comparable homes in the same neighbourhood over the last 60–90 days (your agent can pull these from the MLS), and (3) how many days the listing has been on market. A longer listing period may improve a buyer's negotiating position, but it does not guarantee a price reduction. The SP/LP ratio for that city shows roughly how close recent sales came to their final asking prices, on average. How to read today's pricing in your area →

What does this market data tell buyers, and what does it tell sellers?

For buyers: it shows price trends, inventory and days on market — helping you decide where to search, how quickly to act, and whether there's room to negotiate. A market with more listings than buyers typically gives buyers more time and leverage. For sellers: it shows competition, buyer activity and pricing pressure. If inventory is rising or homes are taking longer to sell, pricing, preparation and presentation matter more. Homes priced accurately sell faster and for more than overpriced homes that sit and get reduced.

How often is this data updated?

The dashboard pulls from TRREB Market Watch, which publishes monthly. Conditions can shift meaningfully from month to month depending on sales activity, new listings, and economic signals like Bank of Canada rate decisions. Always confirm the most recent numbers before relying on them for a purchase or sale decision.
